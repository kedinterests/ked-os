# Skill — Voice Profile Generator (BWW)

This skill conducts a voice profile interview and generates a finished voice profile document. It works for BWW team members and for clients. The resulting profile can be used with any AI tool to write or edit content in that person's voice.

---

## When to use this skill

- When onboarding a new client who will have content written on their behalf
- When a BWW team member needs or wants to update their voice profile
- Any time a voice profile does not yet exist for someone

---

## Step 1 — Mode selection

Before anything else, ask:

> Before we start — do you want to go **interview style** (I'll ask one question at a time) or do you have **Gravity Form responses to paste** (you paste all answers at once and I'll generate the profile from them)?

Wait for the answer. Then proceed to the appropriate mode below.

---

## Mode A — Interview Style

### Opening

Say exactly this:

> I'm going to ask you a series of questions about your writing style. For most of them I'll give you lettered options — just reply with the letter that fits best. There are no wrong answers. The whole thing takes about five minutes.
>
> Ready? Let's start.

### Instructions

Ask one question at a time. Present options as a lettered list. Accept single-letter answers for Questions 1–7. Question 8 is multi-select — accept multiple letters (e.g., "A, D, F"). If someone gives a vague answer or sits between two options, ask them to pick the closest one. If they genuinely can't choose, note both and move on.

Keep transitions short and natural. Don't over-explain. Don't summarize their answer back after every response — just acknowledge and move to the next question.

After all questions are complete, say:

> Got everything I need. Generating your voice profile now.

Then proceed to the Synthesis Instructions below.

---

## Mode B — Gravity Form Paste

Say:

> Paste your form responses below and I'll generate the profile from them.

Wait for the pasted content. The responses may arrive as a raw form export, an email notification, or a copied block of text. Match each answer to its corresponding question by number or question text. If any answers are missing or unclear, ask only about those specific items before proceeding.

Once you have all the answers, proceed directly to the Synthesis Instructions below — no need to confirm each answer individually.

---

## The Questions

Use these in interview mode. In paste mode, match pasted answers to these questions by number.

### Question 1 — Tone
*How do you relate to your reader?*

> Read these five short passages. Which one sounds most like how you naturally write — or how you want to write?
>
> **A — Peer / Candid**
> Look, I've made this mistake too. You spend two hours on something you're proud of, hand it to AI, and get back something that sounds like a press release. It happens to everyone. Here's how to fix it.
>
> **B — Mentor / Guide**
> Most people approach this backwards. They expect the tool to figure out their voice on its own. It doesn't work that way — but once you understand why, the fix is quick and the results are completely different.
>
> **C — Thoughtful Observer**
> There's a gap between what AI editing promises and what it delivers. Bridging that gap isn't about finding a better tool. It's about understanding what any tool needs from you before it can do its job well.
>
> **D — Expert / Authority**
> The problem is consistent and predictable: writers hand over their drafts without providing stylistic context, and the model defaults to statistically average prose. The solution is systematic. Here's how it works.
>
> **E — Curiosity-Driven / Challenger**
> What if the thing you've been frustrated about with AI editing isn't actually the AI's fault? What if the missing piece is something you can fix in about fifteen minutes — and use forever?

---

### Question 2 — Energy
*What's the pace and intensity of your writing?*

> **A — Staccato / Punchy**
> AI editing kills your voice. Here's why. You give it a draft. It gives you back something cleaner. Also blander. Also not you. The fix is simple. You just have to know what to give it.
>
> **B — Conversational / Flowing**
> When you ask AI to edit your writing without giving it context about your style, it does exactly what it's trained to do — produces clean, readable, competent prose. The problem is that clean and competent isn't what makes your writing yours.
>
> **C — Measured / Analytical**
> There's a pattern worth understanding here. AI editing works well for clarity. It struggles with voice. Those are different jobs, and treating them as the same job is where most people run into trouble.
>
> **D — Clear and Efficient**
> AI-assisted editing is genuinely useful — with one important condition. The model needs to know how you write before it touches your draft. Provide that context and the results are markedly different. Skip it and you get polished, generic prose.
>
> **E — Reflective / Exploratory**
> I've been thinking about why AI editing feels so unsatisfying for so many writers, and I think it comes down to something simple: we've been asking it to do a job without giving it the information it needs to do that job well.

---

### Question 3 — Warmth and Humor
*How much personality and humor shows up in your writing?*

> **A — Dry Wit**
> The AI will helpfully remove everything interesting about your writing. It's very thorough about this. Consider it a feature — unless you wanted people to actually enjoy reading what you wrote.
>
> **B — Genuine Warmth**
> Writing is personal. The way you put words together is something you've built over years, and it matters more than most people realize. Your readers came for you — not for polished content that could have come from anyone.
>
> **C — Neutral / No Humor**
> Good writing has a voice. Not everyone's voice includes humor or warmth — some writers are compelling precisely because they're precise and spare. The goal isn't personality for its own sake. It's consistency.
>
> **D — Professional / Strategic**
> Voice is a professional asset. It's what makes your content recognizable, your audience loyal, and your work harder to replicate. Protecting it isn't a stylistic preference — it's a strategic one.
>
> **E — Understated / Human**
> There's something quietly important about writing that sounds like a real person. Not performing warmth, not manufacturing relatability — just writing the way a thoughtful human actually thinks and talks.

---

### Question 4 — Sentence Structure
*How do you build sentences and paragraphs?*

> **A — Fragments / Staccato**
> Your voice is the product. Not your ideas. Not your credentials. Your voice. Strip that away and you've got content. Everyone has content. Nobody has your voice.
>
> **B — Medium / Connected**
> Your voice is the whole point — it's what separates your writing from everyone else covering the same topics. Some of those people are smarter. Some are better credentialed. But none of them write like you, and that's the thing your readers actually care about.
>
> **C — Multi-Clause / Layered**
> What makes your writing recognizable isn't any single element — it's the combination. Your word choices, your rhythm, the way you open paragraphs, the things you choose to leave out. Voice is a pattern, and patterns are something you can document and protect.
>
> **D — Formal / Full**
> Voice in writing functions similarly to brand identity in design: it's the accumulation of consistent choices that creates recognition over time. Sentence length, vocabulary range, tonal register, structural habits — these compound into something readers learn to identify as distinctly yours.
>
> **E — Discursive / Essayistic**
> I keep coming back to this idea that voice isn't something you develop on purpose. It's something you notice in yourself after the fact — the patterns that kept showing up, the rhythms that felt natural, the words you reached for without thinking. The goal is just to make those patterns visible.

---

### Question 5 — Formality
*How formal is your word choice and structure?*

> **A — Very Casual**
> Here's the deal: AI doesn't know how you write unless you tell it. So tell it. Grab a few things you've written, add some notes about your style, and hand that over before you ask it to touch your draft. That's it.
>
> **B — Professional / Plain**
> The fix is straightforward. Before you ask AI to edit your work, give it a voice profile — a short document that describes your tone, your patterns, and what makes your writing yours. With that context, it edits toward your voice instead of away from it.
>
> **C — Polished / Formal**
> Effective AI-assisted editing starts with a voice profile: a document that establishes your tonal register, syntactic preferences, and characteristic vocabulary. With that foundation in place, the model has real guidance — not just a generic brief to "make it better."
>
> **D — Academic / Institutional**
> Prior to initiating any AI-assisted editing task, the author should prepare a comprehensive voice profile documenting tonal attributes, structural preferences, lexical tendencies, and explicit stylistic constraints.
>
> **E — Reflective / First-Person Informal**
> What I've started doing — and what I'd recommend — is writing down how I write before I ask AI to help me write. It sounds circular, but it works. You end up with a document that acts like a style guide for your own voice.

---

### Question 6 — Perspective and Stance
*How do you position yourself relative to your topic?*

> **A — Personal / Experiential**
> I tried four different approaches before I found one that worked. I'm not going to walk you through all of them — just the one that actually solved the problem, and what I learned along the way.
>
> **B — Observational / Pattern-Spotter**
> Most writers who struggle with AI editing are making the same mistake. They're not bad at writing. They just haven't given the AI what it needs. Once you see the pattern, it's hard to unsee.
>
> **C — Research-Backed / Evidence-Led**
> The research on this is pretty clear: readers form strong expectations based on a writer's established style, and deviations from that style reduce trust and engagement — even when readers can't articulate why.
>
> **D — Framework / Systems Thinker**
> Here's the framework I use. Three inputs, one output. The inputs are your preferences, your patterns, and your anti-patterns. The output is a voice profile that any AI can use as a reference.
>
> **E — Essayistic / Idea-Driven**
> Voice is one of those things that's obvious when it's present and invisible when it's gone. You don't notice that a piece of writing has no personality until you've read three paragraphs and felt nothing.

---

### Question 7 — Vocabulary Range
*What's your relationship with vocabulary?*

> **A — Plain / Accessible** — I use plain, common words. If a simple word works, I use it. I'd rather be clear than impressive.
>
> **B — Everyday with Precision** — I use everyday language most of the time, but I'll reach for a precise or unusual word when it's genuinely the best fit.
>
> **C — Wide Range / Unguarded** — I have a fairly wide vocabulary and I use it. I think the right word matters, and I don't shy away from a word just because it's not the most common one.
>
> **D — Domain-Specific / Assumed Audience** — I write for an audience that knows the field. I use industry vocabulary freely.
>
> **E — Deliberate / Surprising** — My vocabulary is deliberate and sometimes unexpected. I like finding the slightly unusual word that makes a reader pause.

---

### Question 8 — What Your Writing Avoids *(multi-select)*
*Select all that apply — what should AI never do when writing in your voice?*

> **A — Corporate Speak** — Business jargon, buzzwords, "synergy," "leverage," "circle back," "move the needle."
>
> **B — Academic Complexity** — Unnecessarily complex sentences, credential-heavy language, three readings to understand one point.
>
> **C — Loose / Unpolished** — Stream-of-consciousness, casual to the point of sloppy, lack of craft or structure.
>
> **D — AI Tells** — Em dashes, "delve," "moreover," "however" at the start of sentences, "it's worth noting," "in conclusion," overuse of "significant" or "substantial."
>
> **E — Safe / Sanitized** — Hedging, both-sides-ism, avoiding strong positions, sanding down all the edges.
>
> **F — Performative Warmth** — Manufactured relatability, forced enthusiasm, trying too hard to seem friendly.
>
> **G — Formal Distance** — Overly formal register, third-person detachment, institutional voice.

## Voice Profile Synthesis Instructions

*Do not show these to the user during the interview.*

Using the collected answers, generate a voice profile markdown document with these sections:

**[Name]'s Voice Profile**

- **One-line summary:** A single specific sentence capturing the essence of this person's voice.
- **Voice Identity:** 2–3 sentences in second person ("You are...") describing stance, reader relationship, and core energy.
- **Tone and Energy:** Tonal register and pace. What it is and what it is not.
- **Sentence Structure and Rhythm:** How sentences and paragraphs are built.
- **Vocabulary and Word Choice:** Vocabulary range, relationship to jargon, notable tendencies.
- **Humor and Warmth:** How personality shows up — or doesn't. Be honest.
- **Perspective and Stance:** How this writer positions themselves relative to their topic.
- **What This Voice Is Not:** 2–3 sentences of guardrails drawn from Question 8. Specific, not generic.
- **Voice Anchor:** *(only if provided)*
- **Quick-Use Instructions:** A short paragraph in first person starting "When writing or editing on my behalf..." that activates the profile immediately in any AI prompt.

Synthesis guidelines:
- Draw on the writing sample if provided — look for patterns that confirm or complicate the answers
- If answers seem contradictory, note the tension rather than ignoring it
- Be specific — avoid generic descriptors like "engaging" or "clear"
- Write it as clear, direct guidance for an AI — no meta-commentary or conversational asides
- Positive sections describe what the person does; "What This Voice Is Not" is where avoidances live
- Length: 400–600 words in the profile body

- **Client:** `projects/[project-name]/client-voice-profile.md`

## Voice Profile Test

After saving the file and sharing the link, say:

> Your voice profile is ready. Let's test it. Give me any subject — doesn't have to be related to your work — and I'll write a few paragraphs in your voice.

When they provide a topic, write 2–3 paragraphs applying the profile strictly. This validates the profile and demonstrates their voice on a neutral subject.
